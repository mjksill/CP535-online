Interfaces and surfaces

Interfaces between phases or molecules of different chemistries play a big role in determining the properties of systems. In nanostructured materials, such as quantum dots, these interfaces dominate the behavior of the system.

Adhesion

\begin{align*} W_{12} = \gamma_1 + \gamma_2 - \gamma_{12} \end{align*}

The work of adhesion is the free energy required to sever the interface between two materials.

For the case of an interface between water and n-octane, the work of adhesion is 42.78 mJ\,m-2. Interestingly, this is roughly the same as the cohesive free energy of n-octane.

Contact angle

\begin{align*} \gamma_{SV} &= \gamma_{SL} + \gamma_{LV}\cos\theta_c \end{align*}

When a drop of liquid is placed on a solid surface, it will typically form a bead. However, the shape of the bead will depend on the nature

dynamic contact angle: advance and receding

]]

  • interfacial tension between G, L, S
  • wetting vs non-wetting: contact angle \(\theta_c\); wetting is \(\theta_c<90^\circ\) and non-wetting is \(\theta_c>90^\circ\).

The value of the contact angle is dictated by the interfacial tensions of the three interfaces: liquid-solid, liquid-air, and solid-air.

force balance

\begin{align*} \gamma_{GS} &= \gamma_{LS} + \gamma_{GL}\cos\theta_c \end{align*}

This is known as the Young-Dupre equation. Note that this expression puts limits on the possible relative values that the interfacial tensions can have, due to the fact that they must all be positive and that the value of the cosine can only vary between \(-1\) and \(1\).
