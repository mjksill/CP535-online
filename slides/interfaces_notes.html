Interfacial / surface tension

Let's examine an interface between air and water. There is fairly strong attractive interaction between water molecule; it is this attraction that leads water molecules to aggregate together to form a liquid, at room temperature and atmospheric pressure.

Let's examine the forces acting on a water molecule. Within the bulk of the liquid, we see that a water molecule is fully surrounded by other water molecules. It is equally attracted to each of these molecules, and because these molecules are uniformly distributed (on average) around it, there is a balance of forces. There is no net tendency for it to move in any particular direction.

Now let's consider a water molecule near the interface. It still feels the attraction of the other water molecules towards the left and right and towards the interior of the liquid phase; however, because the lack of molecules in the air, due to its much lower density and the weaker attraction of water for these species, there is no pull of the water molecule into the gas phase. As a result, there is an imbalance of forces on water molecules near the surface which tends to pull them back into the bulk of the liquid. This is only stopped by the excluded volume interactions between molecules (i.e. the tendency for molecules not to overlap each other).

Surface tension

\begin{align*} dW &= \gamma dA \\ dW &= {\bf F} \cdot d{\bf r} \end{align*}

The macroscopic consequence of this lack of attractive interactions due to the presence of a surface is that it appears that liquids try to minimize their surface area. This is why water droplets tend to be spherical, as a sphere is the shape that has the minimum surface area for a given volume.

To quantify this physical observation, we say that there is an energy cost, or more precisely, a free energy cost, for creating an interface, which we expect to be proportional to the area of the interface. We call this the surface/interfacial free energy. This is the free energy (or work) per unit area required to create an interface. That is \(dW = \gamma dA\), where \(\gamma\) is the interfacial free energy.

In order to get a better physical feel of what this means, consider a thin film of liquid that is being stretched across a wire frame. Across the frame is a moveable link of length \(l\), which can be used to increase or decrease the area of the liquid film.

If we move the link by a small distance \(dx\) to the right, then the area of the liquid film will increase by \(2ldx\) (note that there are two surface on the film: the upper surface and the lower surface). From our ideas about the interfacial free energy, we say that the work required to form this additional amount of interface is \(\gamma 2l dx\).

Recall that the mechanical work performed by a force is equal to its magnitude multiplied by the displacement over which it acts.

As a result of this link between work and force, the interfacial free energy is also known as the interfacial tension, which has units of force per unit length.

Cohesion

\begin{align*} W_{11} &= 2\gamma_1 \end{align*}

The first concept we want to introduce is the cohesion free energy or the work of cohesion. This is the work required to take a bulk material and cut it into two pieces. This is a measure of the strength of the intermolecular forces that hold a material together.

In cutting a material in half, we just form two interfaces. Per cross-sectional area, this work is just twice the surface tension of the material. So, surface tension can be thought of as characterizing the strength of a material.

For water, we have a cohesion free energy of 145.6 mJ\,m-2, while for n-octane we have 43.24 mJ\,m-2.

Adhesion

\begin{align*} W_{12} = \gamma_1 + \gamma_2 - \gamma_{12} \end{align*}

The work of adhesion is the free energy required to sever the interface between two materials. This gives an indication of how strongly the two different materials want to adhere to each other.

When we cut the interface between to materials, we lose the interfacial free energy between to two interfaces, and we gain the surface tensions of the two separate materials.

For the case of an interface between water and n-octane, the work of adhesion is 42.78 mJ\,m-2. Interestingly, this is roughly the same as the cohesive free energy of n-octane.

For mercury and water, the work of adhesion is 83.21 mJ m-2.
